## Recognizing a True Pet Emergency
Not every minor issue requires a 3:00 AM visit, but certain symptoms are absolutely non-negotiable and require immediate intervention. You should seek emergency care if your pet experiences:
*   Difficulty breathing, choking, or pale/blue gums
*   Unexplained bleeding or trauma (like being hit by a car)
*   Seizures lasting more than a minute, or cluster seizures
*   Inability to urinate (this is especially common and rapidly fatal in male cats)
*   Suspected ingestion of toxins (chocolate, xylitol, grapes, rat poison, or human medications)
*   Signs of bloat (unproductive retching and a hard, distended abdomen in dogs)

## Understanding the ER Triage Process
When you walk through the doors of a veterinary ER, it operates exactly like a human hospital: **pets are treated based on the severity of their medical condition, not on a first-come, first-served basis.**

1.  **Immediate Assessment:** A veterinary technician will quickly evaluate your pet’s vitals (heart rate, breathing effort, mucous membrane color).
2.  **Stabilization:** If your pet is deemed critical, they will be taken to the back treatment area immediately for life-saving measures like oxygen therapy or pain management, often before you even sign the paperwork.
3.  **Consultation:** Once stable, the ER vet will sit down with you to discuss diagnostic options, prognosis, and the treatment plan.
